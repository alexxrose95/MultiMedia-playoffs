A Doorway to the Premier League

The Championship Playoffs

By Cai Thomas and Alex Rose

History of the Owls

The system of the play-offs was first implemented in 1987, when the EFL Championship was formerly known as the Second Division of the Football league. Charlton Athletic became the first team to benefit from the newly founded route into the First Division of the Football League as they beat Leeds United in the final.

The promotion system remained unchanged up until 1993 – the year the First Division was rebranded into the FA Premier League. The change at the top flight level had a knock-on effect on the tier below as the Second Division play-offs was renamed as the Division One Play-Offs.

Then, in 2005, the system was altered once more. The First Division Play-Offs as it was formerly known back then, was rebranded again and changed to the EFL Championship Play-Offs; the system that is still to this day utilised in order to designate the third promotion slot.

How Does It Work?

The EFL Championship’s play-off system is fairly straightforward; the four teams who miss out on the top two automatic promotion places battle it out for a place in Wembley and, more importantly, a place in the Premier League. In order to have the opportunity to face the big guns of English football the following season, one of the sides must be victorious over two ties. Whoever finishes 3rd plays the side that finished 6th – meaning 4th comes up against 5th. Both of these fixtures are played over two legs at the clubs’ respective stadiums. Once both ties are settled, the final will be played at Wembley where the winner will be crowned play-off champions and promoted into the Premier League.

‘The Richest Game In Football’

Despite being arguably the most complicated route into the Premier League, the play-offs makes up for it in revenue. It has been described as the most valuable sporting event in the world with £60 million pounds up for grabs to the winner. As a courtesy, though, the runners up normally keep the ticket revenue of the day; a consolation prize of some sort.

The massive amount of prize money at stake is so beneficial that it can sometimes mean it’s better to get promoted via the play-offs. On seven different occasions, the play-off winners have finished above the winner and runner up in the following season; magnifying the significance of the jackpot the play-off route has to offer.

Despite often being branded the ‘100 million pound game’, the ‘Mail on Sunday’ recently published a report on how much money the play-offs actually generate.
